# LANDING-PAGE.md — Direct Response Landing Page Framework
# USE ONLY FOR: Single-page conversion campaigns
# DO NOT USE FOR: Full multi-page websites (use SEO-ARCHITECTURE.md instead)

## Overview

A landing page is a single-purpose conversion machine. It has one audience, one offer,
one primary CTA, and minimal (or no) navigation away from the page. Every element exists
to move the visitor toward the desired action.

This skill implements a 10-section direct-response framework. Not every landing page
needs all 10 sections — the brief will indicate which sections to include. But the
ORDER matters. The psychological flow is: hook → agitate → solve → prove → offer → close.

## The 10-Section Framework

### Section 1: Hero
**Purpose:** Stop the scroll. Make the visitor feel "this is for me" in 3 seconds.

Elements:
- **Headline:** The big promise. Benefit-driven, specific. Under 12 words ideal.
- **Subheadline:** The USP. How you deliver the promise differently than anyone else.
- **Primary CTA:** Clear, action-oriented. Uses the verb the visitor wants to do ("Get", "Start", "Book", "Claim").
- **Social proof bar** (optional): Trust signals below the CTA — customer count, ratings, logos, credentials.

Rules:
- Headline must pass the "billboard test" — would someone driving by at 60mph understand it?
- No jargon in the hero. Save technical details for later sections.
- CTA button text should NOT be generic ("Submit", "Learn More"). It should state the outcome ("Get My Free Quote", "Book My Demo").
- If the page targets multiple products/offers, the hero can have 2 CTAs max — but each must be clearly labeled.

### Section 2: Problem & Pain Agitation
**Purpose:** Make the visitor feel seen. Show you understand their world before pitching.

Structure:
- **Empathy opener:** Start with "Let me guess..." or "If you're like most [target]..." — show you know their situation.
- **Pain bullets (4-5):** List specific frustrations. Use their language, not industry jargon.
- **Agitation closer:** "Sound familiar?" or "You're not alone — but you don't have to stay stuck."

Rules:
- Never insult the visitor or their current approach. Empathize, don't condescend.
- Pain points should come from the brief's buyer persona (objections, barriers, frustrations).
- Be specific. "Your costs are rising" is weak. "Your CPL climbed 30% this year while close rates stayed flat" is strong.

### Section 3: Introduce the Solution (USP + Benefits)
**Purpose:** Position your product/service as the answer to the pain just described.

Structure:
- **Headline:** "That's why we built [Product]" or "There's a better way."
- **Subheadline:** Social proof reinforcement — "We've helped [X] businesses like yours..."
- **Visual progression** (optional): A before-after bar or flow diagram showing the transformation.
- **USP pillars:** 3-5 glass cards or icon blocks, each with a short benefit statement.
- **Outcome list:** What the customer actually gets as a result (more leads, lower costs, faster response, etc.)

Rules:
- Lead with outcomes, not features. "24/7 response" is a feature. "Never lose a lead to voicemail again" is an outcome.
- Don't list everything you do. List what matters to THIS audience based on the persona.

### Section 4: How It Works (Step by Step)
**Purpose:** Remove uncertainty. Show the process is simple and low-risk.

Structure:
- 3-5 numbered steps with short titles and 1-sentence descriptions
- Timeline if applicable ("Days 1-3: Setup. Week 1: Launch.")

Rules:
- Steps should feel fast and easy. If the process is complex, simplify how you describe it.
- End the last step with the desired outcome, not an action ("Start seeing results" not "Submit your data").

### Section 5: Social Proof & Case Studies
**Purpose:** Prove it works with evidence.

Structure:
- **Stat cards:** 3 bold numbers with context (e.g., "8x higher qualification with <5-minute responses")
- **Case study cards:** 2-3 brief stories (one sentence each with a result number)
- **Testimonial** (optional): One strong quote with name/role/company

Rules:
- Numbers beat adjectives. "+22% more appointments" beats "dramatically improved results."
- If testimonials are not available (noted in brief), use industry benchmark data instead.
- Never fabricate testimonials or case studies. Use the brief's proof points or cite industry data with attribution.

### Section 6: Who This Is For
**Purpose:** Qualification. Let the right visitor self-identify.

Structure:
- Short list of roles, business types, or situations that fit
- Can be presented as tags/pills, a short paragraph, or "This is for you if..." bullet list

Rules:
- Be specific enough to qualify but broad enough not to exclude good prospects.
- Derived directly from the brief's target audience and buyer persona.

### Section 7: What You Actually Get
**Purpose:** Deliverables list. Remove ambiguity about what's included.

Structure:
- Organized by product/package if multiple offerings
- Bullet list of specific deliverables with brief descriptions
- Can include feature comparison table if multiple tiers

Rules:
- Every deliverable should be tangible and understandable.
- "AI-powered ad optimization" is vague. "Daily automated adjustments to your ad targeting and budget allocation" is clear.

### Section 8: Pricing & Offers
**Purpose:** Convert interest into action with a compelling offer.

Structure:
- **Hook card** (optional): Lead with the biggest value proposition ("Get [X] FREE when you...")
- **Pricing cards:** 1-3 tiers in a grid
- Each card: headline, subheadline, normal price (strikethrough if discounted), offer price, what's included, CTA
- **Guarantee badge:** Risk-reversal statement
- **Savings callout:** Make the math obvious

Pricing Psychology Rules:
- **Anchoring:** Show the industry standard or normal price first (in strikethrough) before showing your price.
- **Savings math:** Calculate and display the exact dollar savings. Don't make the visitor do math.
- **Tiered pricing:** If 3 tiers, highlight the middle tier as "Most Popular" or "Best Value."
- **Risk reversal:** Always include a guarantee. "60-Day Guarantee" or "Money-back if no results."
- **Urgency** (if authentic): Limited spots, time-limited pricing, or enrollment caps.

### Section 9: FAQ
**Purpose:** Handle every remaining objection before they leave.

Structure:
- Accordion-style Q&A (8-12 questions)
- Questions sourced from the brief's buyer persona objections, barriers, and common concerns

Rules:
- Title this section "FAQ" — not "Frequently Asked Questions" (wastes space) or "Health FAQ" or anything clever.
- Every question should be a real objection a buyer would have, not softball questions.
- Answers should be concise (2-3 sentences) and confident. Don't hedge.
- Include schema.org FAQPage markup even on landing pages — this helps with AI search visibility.

### Section 10: Final CTA + Urgency
**Purpose:** Close the deal. Last chance before they leave.

Structure:
- **Headline:** Restate the core value or create urgency ("Don't get left behind")
- **Copy:** 1-2 sentences reinforcing the offer or scarcity
- **Primary CTA button:** Same action as the hero CTA
- **Secondary micro-CTA:** Phone number or alternative contact method

Rules:
- This section should feel like a conclusion, not a repeat. Don't rehash the entire pitch.
- If there's a legitimate enrollment cap or deadline, state it clearly.
- Never use fake scarcity. If it's not real, don't claim it.

## Landing Page Navigation Rules

- **No full site navigation.** A landing page should not have links to unrelated pages.
- **Sticky nav** (optional): Anchor links to sections on the same page only (Problem, Solution, Pricing, FAQ, etc.)
- **Logo** links back to top of page, not to a separate homepage.
- **Mobile:** Sticky bottom CTA bar on mobile (fixed position, always visible).

## Landing Page vs Website: Key Differences

| Aspect | Landing Page | Full Website |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Pages | 1 | Multiple |
| Navigation | None or anchor-only | Full site nav |
| Audience | Single persona/offer | Multiple audiences |
| CTA | One primary action | Multiple goals per page |
| SEO approach | One keyword cluster | Silo architecture |
| Content tone | Persuasive/conversion | Informational + conversion |
| Internal linking | None (single page) | Extensive cross-linking |

## Email Sequence Integration

Landing pages are often paired with email campaigns. If the brief includes an email
sequence component, follow this structure:

### 3-Email Sequence Pattern
- **Email 1 (Day 1) — Hook:** Empathy + pain agitation + introduce the solution. Light CTA.
- **Email 2 (Day 2) — Proof:** Case studies + results + offer details. Medium CTA.
- **Email 3 (Day 3) — Urgency:** Scarcity + ROI math + final call to action. Hard CTA.

### Email Design Rules
- Plain-text or near-plain-text format (not designed HTML templates)
- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max)
- Bold for emphasis on numbers and offers only
- CTA as a text link, not a giant button (builds trust, improves deliverability)
- P.S. line at the bottom for urgency/guarantee reminder (gets disproportionate readership)
- Personalization: [First Name] merge tags where available

## Buyer Persona Integration

Every landing page must be written FOR a specific buyer persona from the brief.
The persona informs:

- **Hero headline:** Speaks to the persona's primary goal
- **Problem section:** Lists the persona's specific pain points
- **Solution section:** Addresses the persona's objections preemptively
- **Pricing section:** Anchored against what this persona currently pays
- **FAQ section:** Answers this persona's specific objections and barriers
- **CTA language:** Uses verbs and outcomes relevant to this persona's role

If the landing page targets multiple personas, the hero must be broad enough to
resonate with all of them, but the problem/solution sections can address different
personas in sequence (using clear subheadings).
